# Helping Ralph focus

Some thoughts for the day.

- Vibe coding is just "coding" now. It's all vibes. Move on.
- It's hard to focus on what really matters, no matter who you are.
- The software really is basically free now.
- I'm still using [Ralph Wiggum Loops](https://ghuntley.com/loop/). More than ever, actually.
- Design still matters. For now.

I'm building a software tool. For the sake of argument, let's say it's a cupcake design app. It helps you quickly design recipes and making shopping lists. The goal is to get you out of the software and into the kitchen as fast as possible. I'm excited about it. 

I have big dreams of big features.

I can now make a giant spec with all of those features, feed it to an AI, and use a Ralph Wiggum loop to Just Keep Building until software comes out the other end.

I can gate it so that certain quality metrics have to be met before the loop stops. I can force it to meet accessibility, security, performance, and testing requirements.

I can even ask it to Think Really Hard about how to make this a great experience, and that can actually be helpful and clarifying. (I often ask the AI to interview me, asking questions one at a time to help explain and validate my ideas. Basically [rubber ducking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber_duck_debugging) my ideas.)

I can build great steaming heaps of software this way and it's a glorious mess.

But it still costs a lot of time and tokens to build complicated features. And so far LLMs are still not very good at designing UI.

But the biggest limiting factor is focus. It's easy to shoot software out the slop cannon. It's harder to think of something genuinely novel, useful, and wears its value on its sleeve.

So as I build this cupcake design app, I have to discipline myself to focus on the real goals of the app: how do I get to a shopping list as quickly as possible. It's so tempting to pull out the slop cannon and say "PDF export! Splap! Automatic unit conversions! Splow! Convection oven conversion tables! Splop!" But without a focused, coherent message, it's just a heap of features that nobody will ever find.

In other words, I need to design it. 

Not the pixels, mind. I just need to distill the core ideas down to the truly valuable golden path and build that first, and resist the urge to build out the edge cases. They're easy to add later.

AI can help with this. I can ask it to interview me about the product and suggest UX flows. I can ask it to detail those UX flows textually, and define finite state machines that describe the states of the UI. I can ask it to turn all of that into a detailed implementation plan that uses standard UX affordances and best practices for clarity and accessibility. I can ask it to interview me about the intended information hierarchy and help me narrow down an approach. 

It doesn't matter how I do it. I just have to make sure it gets done--preferably before building out the features.

I might not need this discipline forever. It won't be long before stronger AI, equipped with great design skills, will be able to do all of this automatically. And it's funny to be cynical and imagine us turning into Wall-E people in hoverchairs, shouting "make me a cupcake app" and "make it more gooder" between slurps of our slurpees. But maybe it's just standing in the kitchen, baking cupcakes, saying "Hey Siri, make me an app that helps me design cupcake recipes and make shopping lists," and the app gets built before I finish baking. 

It wouldn't be a bad thing to spend less time designing and coding tools and more time focusing on what matters.
